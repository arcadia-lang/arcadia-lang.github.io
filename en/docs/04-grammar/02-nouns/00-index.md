# Nouns

Nouns form the foundation of Arcadia's nominal grammar.

Every noun belongs to a grammatical class, has a countability profile, and may inflect for grammatical number and case.

## Specificity

Arcadia distinguishes nouns by their default specificity.

- **Proper nouns** directly identify particular referents, such as _Arcadia_ or _Maria_.
- **Common nouns** denote kinds, classes, or concepts, such as _tree_, _apple_, or _chastity_.

A proper noun requires no article when it has its default specific interpretation.
An article may override this default specificity.
An indefinite article before _Maria_ refers to an unspecified person called Maria rather than to the particular person identified by the bare name.

Further details are provided in the [articles guide][articles].

## Gender

Arcadia is not a gendered language.
Gender is not an inherent grammatical property of nouns and does not control agreement.
Optional gender prefixes may be used when gender is relevant to the intended meaning.
These prefixes function as contextual derivational markers rather than obligatory grammatical features.

Further details are provided in the [gender guide][gender].

## Grammatical Classes

Every noun belongs to one of five grammatical [classes][class].
A noun's class is identified by the word-final vowel of its citation form.

The five classes are:

| Class  | Typical members                                                                                        |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **-i** | Tools, artefacts, and manufactured objects                                                             |
| **-e** | People, animals, plants, and other living organisms                                                    |
| **-a** | Abstract concepts, qualities, and events                                                               |
| **-o** | Natural objects, materials, and places                                                                 |
| **-u** | Consonant-final acronyms, initialisms, foreign words, and technical terms after the addition of **-u** |

These associations are semantic tendencies rather than strict definitions.
A noun's grammatical class is part of its lexical identity and cannot be inferred solely from its meaning.

The class system primarily supports agreement and noun formation.
Newly coined native nouns are normally assigned a class by semantic analogy.
A vowel-final acronym, initialism, foreign word, or technical term retains its form and takes the class identified by its word-final vowel.
A consonant-final acronym, initialism, foreign word, or technical term receives final **-u** and therefore enters the **-u class**.
A borrowed noun may later become lexicalised in another class as it becomes integrated into the Arcadian vocabulary.
Every noun must end in a vowel.
Only the word-final vowel identifies its class.
A noun ending in **-ia** therefore belongs to the **-a class**.

## Countability

Arcadia distinguishes nouns by countability.

- **Countable nouns** refer to entities lexicalised as discrete instances that can be enumerated, such as _apples_ or _hours_.
- **Uncountable nouns** refer to substances, abstractions, or continua that are not lexicalised as discrete instances, such as _water_ or _love_.

Countability is a lexical property of the noun.

Derivational morphology may convert a countable noun into an uncountable noun or an uncountable noun into a countable noun.

Further details are provided in the [new noun formation guide][new-nouns].

## Grammatical Number

Arcadia distinguishes five grammatical [numbers][number]:

- **Singular** marks exactly one complete instance.
- **Fractional** marks an incomplete part of one instance.
- **Plural** marks more than one instance without asserting completeness.
- **Nullar** marks the absence of any instance.
- **Total** marks the complete set of contextually relevant instances.

The fractional number ends phonologically in **/d/**.
This final voiced obstruent is preserved as an explicit morphological exception to final devoicing.

## Case

Arcadia uses semantic case marking to identify the role of a noun phrase within a clause.
Case applies to the entire noun phrase but is realised on its overt head.
When a noun is the head of the phrase, the case marker attaches directly to the noun.
The resulting form behaves as an inflected noun rather than as a separate postpositional phrase.
Adjectives that modify a noun do not inherit its case.
An adjective receives case only when it functions as the head of a noun phrase in its own right.
In a coordinated noun phrase, case is realised on every conjunct component of its single compound head.

Further details are provided in the [case guide][case].
General phrase-head and coordination rules are provided in the [phrase structure guide][phrase-structure].
