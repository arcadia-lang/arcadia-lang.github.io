# Orthography

Arcadia uses a Latin-based orthography with predictable grapheme-to-phoneme correspondence.
Native spelling is designed to be readable, regular, and compatible with plain ASCII input.

## General Principles

Native Arcadian orthography follows these principles:

- each native grapheme has a predictable pronunciation;
- there are no silent letters in native words;
- native text is written in lowercase;
- capitalisation is not used for native grammatical purposes;
- foreign names, standardised symbols, and technical abbreviations may keep their source spelling;
- stress is marked only when it is not penultimate;
- vowel length is normally not written, because it is predictable from stress.

## Character Set

Arcadia uses the Latin letters below in native orthographic material:

```text
a b c d e f g h i j l m n o p r s t u v x y z
```

The letters ⟨k⟩, ⟨q⟩, and ⟨w⟩ are not part of the native Arcadian character set.

Although ⟨h⟩, ⟨x⟩, and ⟨y⟩ belong to the character set, they are not independent native graphemes.
In native spelling, they occur only as the second letter of a digraph.

## Independent Graphemes

The following letters function as independent native graphemes:

```text
a b c d e f g i j l m n o p r s t u v z
```

The following letters do not function independently in native words:

```text
h x y
```

## Digraphs

Arcadia uses digraphs for phonemes not represented by a single independent letter.

```text
ch dh gh sh th zh dx tx ly ny
```

The digraphs are treated as single graphemes for pronunciation and phonological analysis.

## Stress Marking

Monosyllabic native words are stressed on their only syllable and do not require an accent mark.

In multisyllabic native words, penultimate stress is the default.
Words with penultimate stress are written without a stress mark.

Words with non-penultimate stress take an acute accent on the stressed vowel:

```text
á é í ó ú
```

The acute accent marks stress only.
It does not mark vowel quality.

## Stress and Vowel Length

The stressed vowel is phonetically long.
Unstressed vowels are phonetically short.

Because vowel length is predictable from stress, vowel length is not normally written in the orthography.

In phonetic transcription, stressed vowels may be written with the length mark [ː].
In phonemic or orthographic discussion, length is normally omitted unless the distinction between phonemic and phonetic representation is relevant.

## Vowel Merging

Adjacent identical vowels merge into a single long vowel in careful grammatical description.

A macron may be used in dictionaries, paradigms, or grammatical references to show a merged long vowel:

```text
ā ē ī ō ū
```

The macron is not part of ordinary running text.
In ordinary spelling, merged vowels may be written in simplified form unless a grammatical reference requires the merger to be shown explicitly.

## Gemination

Consonant gemination is written by doubling the relevant consonant letter.

For single-letter graphemes, the letter is doubled.

For digraphs, only the final letter of the digraph is doubled.
The first letter remains unchanged so that the base digraph remains recognisable.

## Native and Non-Native Spelling

Native Arcadian words follow native orthographic restrictions.

Proper names, international names, standardised symbols, scientific units, and technical abbreviations may retain non-native characters, capitalisation, or spelling conventions.

When a non-native spelling is retained, pronunciation may be supplied with IPA where clarity is needed.

## Orthographic Normalisation

For normalised native text:

- use lowercase letters;
- use native graphemes only;
- omit capitalisation;
- mark only non-penultimate stress;
- do not write predictable vowel length;
- do not use standalone ⟨h⟩, ⟨x⟩, or ⟨y⟩ in native words;
- reserve ⟨k⟩, ⟨q⟩, and ⟨w⟩ for non-native material only.
