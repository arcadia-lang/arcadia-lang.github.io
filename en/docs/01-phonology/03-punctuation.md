# Punctuation

Arcadian punctuation is conservative and functional.
It marks sentence type, clause structure, quotation, and list structure without interacting with capitalisation.

## Punctuation Marks

| Mark | Name             | Usage                                                                                        |
| ---- | ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `.`  | Period           | Ends a declarative sentence.                                                                 |
| `;`  | Semicolon        | Connects closely related independent clauses or separates complex list items.                |
| `,`  | Comma            | Separates list items, non-final clauses, parenthetical material, and light syntactic breaks. |
| `:`  | Colon            | Introduces an explanation, quotation, formal statement, list, or expansion.                  |
| `!`  | Exclamation mark | Marks strong emotion, urgency, emphasis, or command force.                                   |
| `?`  | Question mark    | Marks a direct question.                                                                     |
| `""` | Quotation marks  | Enclose direct speech, quoted material, titles, or cited forms.                              |

## Spacing

In normalised text:

- no space appears before a punctuation mark;
- one space appears after sentence-final punctuation;
- one space appears after commas, semicolons, and colons;
- quotation marks are placed directly beside the material they enclose.

## Sentence-Final Punctuation

A sentence normally takes exactly one final punctuation mark.

When quoted material occurs at the end of a sentence, duplicated final punctuation is avoided.
If the quoted material and the surrounding sentence would end with the same punctuation mark, the mark is written once, outside the quotation.

If the quoted material requires a different punctuation mark from the surrounding sentence, the mark belonging to the quoted material remains inside the quotation, and the mark belonging to the surrounding sentence remains outside.

## Comma

The comma marks a minor syntactic boundary.
It may separate:

- coordinated items;
- secondary and other dependent clauses;
- introductory material;
- parenthetical phrases;
- vocative elements;
- appositional material.

Commas should not be used where a stronger boundary is required.

## Semicolon

The semicolon marks a boundary stronger than a comma but weaker than a period.

It may join independent clauses when the second clause continues, contrasts with, or clarifies the first.
It may also separate list items that are internally complex.

## Colon

The colon introduces material that expands, explains, defines, enumerates, or formally presents what precedes it.

It may introduce quoted speech, but quotation marks are still used when the quoted material is direct.

## Question Mark

The question mark marks direct questions.

Indirect questions do not require a question mark unless the full sentence is itself interrogative.

## Exclamation Mark

The exclamation mark marks heightened force.

It should be used sparingly in formal or neutral prose.

## Quotation Marks

Double quotation marks are the default quotation marks.

Quotation marks may enclose:

- direct speech;
- cited wording;
- titles;
- orthographic forms under discussion;
- metalinguistic references.

For linguistic notation, IPA and orthographic brackets are preferred where precision is needed.

## Capitalisation

Native Arcadian punctuation does not trigger capitalisation.

Sentence beginnings, names, titles, and quoted material remain lowercase in normalised native text unless non-native spelling conventions are intentionally retained.
