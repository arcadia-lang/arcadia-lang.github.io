# Phrase Structure

Every phrase has exactly one overt head.
A grammatical marker attaches to the head of a phrase while taking scope over the complete phrase.
The realisation of the marker depends on the lexical category of the head.

## Marker Realisation

When a noun or an adjective functioning nominally heads a phrase, its case marker attaches directly and surfaces as an inflectional case ending.
When a category that does not inflect for case heads a phrase, such as a preposition or verb, the same marker is realised as a detached clitic.

## Coordinated Phrases

A coordinated phrase has a single compound head at phrase level.
Each conjunct contributes an overt component to the compound head.
The compound head counts as the one head of the complete phrase.
A marker that takes scope over the complete coordinated phrase is realised on every conjunct component.
The conjunction binds the marked conjuncts into one syntactic unit.

## Nominal Heads

Adjectives do not inherit the case of the noun phrase they modify.
They agree only with the grammatical class of their nominal head.
This keeps the phrase head overtly identifiable.

An adjective may itself head a noun phrase.
In that use, it receives case marking directly:

- _the pregnant woman_: the adjective modifies a case-marked noun;
- _the pregnant_: the adjective is the case-marked head of the noun phrase.

Case therefore identifies both the semantic role of a phrase and the overt head through which that phrase participates in the clause.
